Generating a Schrödinger-cat-like state
  • By injecting two atoms with opposite phases in a high-Q cavity repeatedly, we can generate an optical Schrödinger-cat-like state in the cavity. Two-level atoms are prepared in a superposition state of the ground and excited states but they have opposite phases in the superposition. If the cavity field were classical, the emitted waves from two atoms would cancel each other exactly because of the opposite phase. However, in quantum mechanics, they do not cancel each other and the resulting cavity field evolves to a squeezed vacuum instead. Interesting thing happens when a single photon decay out of the cavity. This decay process substracts a photon from the squeezed vacuum state. The resulting state is approximately given by a superposition of two squeezed states with opposite phases, resembling a Schrödinger cat state. Note that the single photon decay here serves as a herald for the cat state. By detecting a photon from the cavity, we can be sure that there exists a cat state in the cavity.
In this example, the mean photon number associated with a squeezed vacuum state is 4.6. A photon decay makes the state collapse to a cat-like state with a mean photon number of 12.9.
